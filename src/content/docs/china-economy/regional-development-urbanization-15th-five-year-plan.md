China's 15th Five-Year Plan treats geography as economic policy. The document's regional and urbanization sections are not only about where people live. They are about how China organizes production, public services, infrastructure, innovation, land, energy, food security, ecological protection, and domestic demand across a vast country.

For English readers, the most important point is that China's regional strategy is not one policy. It is a layered map: east, central, west, northeast; major city clusters; river basins; border regions; coastal and inland corridors; county economies; functional zones; and people-centered urbanization.

## The short answer

Regional development in China's 15th Five-Year Plan means using city clusters, regional corridors, urbanization reform, land-use controls, infrastructure links, and cross-region cooperation to improve the domestic growth map. The plan wants major regions to specialize, city clusters to become stronger growth engines, inland and border regions to connect better, and migrant workers to gain more urban public services.

The plan has five regional layers:

| Layer | What it does |
| --- | --- |
| Macro-regions | West, northeast, central, and east each receive different development roles. |
| Major city clusters | Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei, Yangtze River Delta, Greater Bay Area, Chengdu-Chongqing, and other clusters act as growth poles. |
| Cross-region corridors | Transport, power, data, logistics, river-basin, and border corridors expand domestic circulation. |
| Functional zones | Urbanized areas, agricultural production zones, ecological zones, and special-function areas receive different policy constraints. |
| People-centered urbanization | Hukou reform, public services, housing, education, and social insurance follow migrant populations more closely. |

The plan's regional story is about making the domestic market more integrated without making every region do the same thing.

## Why regional policy matters now

China's economy is large enough that internal geography can act like an external market. A better-connected domestic market can support demand, specialization, labor mobility, innovation, and supply-chain resilience.

But geography also creates friction:

- coastal regions are more globally integrated than many inland areas;
- local protection can split markets;
- public services do not always follow migrant workers;
- some cities face population inflows while others shrink;
- regions compete for similar industrial projects;
- ecological and food-security regions cannot be evaluated only by GDP;
- infrastructure can either connect markets or create debt-heavy duplication.

The 15th Five-Year Plan tries to manage those frictions through a more coordinated spatial strategy.

## The four macro-regions have different jobs

The plan describes different roles for China's broad regions.

| Region | Policy role in the plan |
| --- | --- |
| West | High-quality development through ecological protection, opening channels, hubs, and distinctive industries. |
| Northeast | Revitalization tied to national defense, food, ecology, energy, industrial security, and northward opening. |
| Central | Rise through food and energy raw-material security, advanced equipment, high-tech industry, transport hubs, and inland opening. |
| East | Lead modernization, original innovation, advanced manufacturing clusters, and modern service hubs. |

This is not equalization by making every region identical. It is differentiated development. The plan wants regions to use comparative advantage while reducing severe imbalance.

## City clusters are the main growth architecture

China's regional strategy increasingly runs through city clusters and metropolitan areas. The plan highlights Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei, the Yangtze River Delta, the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area, Chengdu-Chongqing, and other city clusters.

The logic is that modern growth needs dense networks:

- labor markets;
- universities;
- suppliers;
- ports and airports;
- high-speed rail;
- research platforms;
- logistics;
- services;
- capital;
- public institutions.

Single cities matter, but clusters can share functions. A core city may handle finance, headquarters, research, and global links, while nearby cities provide manufacturing, housing, logistics, and specialized services.

The plan's metropolitan language points to commuter circles, industrial circles, and shared living circles. That is a practical urbanization agenda: connect jobs, homes, services, and transport across administrative boundaries.

## Hukou reform is still central

The plan calls for people-centered urbanization and more integration of rural migrants into cities. It mentions household-registration reform, public household accounts at street or community level, more channels for migrants with stable jobs and housing to settle, and a gradual move toward residence-based population management.

It also calls for:

- more migrant workers joining urban employee social insurance;
- eligible non-registered residents receiving public rental housing support;
- easier school access for migrant children;
- stronger links between people, land, and fiscal resources;
- protection of rural land rights for migrants who settle in cities.

This is one of the most important parts of the plan for domestic demand. Migrant workers who lack urban public services save and spend differently from registered urban residents. If public services follow people more effectively, consumption and labor mobility can improve.

## Land and functional zones constrain growth

The plan also emphasizes national land-space governance. This includes farmland protection, ecological red lines, urban development boundaries, strategic mineral exploration, coastal and marine space, and differentiated policy for functional zones.

That means not every region should chase the same GDP model. Some areas are supposed to concentrate population and industry. Some are supposed to protect food production. Some are supposed to protect ecology. Some carry additional security, energy, cultural, or marine functions.

This creates a harder but more realistic planning framework. Development is not only about building more. It is about matching industry, population, land, water, ecology, and security constraints.

## Cross-region cooperation is a market-integration tool

The plan calls for cross-region infrastructure, industrial coordination, benefit sharing, tax and land index sharing, carbon quota coordination, regional legislation exploration, and compensation between upstream and downstream areas or resource exporters and importers.

These details matter because China's administrative boundaries can block economic integration. A factory, data center, grid line, logistics route, river-basin project, or commuter rail line may create benefits across jurisdictions. If costs and benefits are not shared, projects become harder.

The plan wants mechanisms that make cross-region cooperation more normal.

## What to watch

The regional agenda will be visible in local plans and infrastructure choices. Watch:

- whether city clusters reduce administrative barriers or only add slogans;
- whether hukou reform expands real access to education, housing, and social insurance;
- whether inland regions gain stronger logistics and industrial links;
- whether regional industrial policy avoids duplicating the same sectors everywhere;
- whether ecological and agricultural zones receive enough fiscal compensation;
- whether cross-region benefit-sharing mechanisms become operational;
- whether urbanization improves household confidence and consumption.

## FAQ

### What is people-centered urbanization?

It means urbanization measured not only by buildings and city boundaries, but by whether people who live and work in cities can access public services, housing, education, social insurance, and stable rights.

### Why do city clusters matter?

Because modern industries rely on networks of labor, suppliers, research, logistics, finance, and services. City clusters can coordinate those networks better than isolated cities.

### Is China still encouraging migration to big cities?

The plan supports urbanization, but it also wants a more balanced city system, stronger metropolitan areas, and better integration of migrants into cities where they already live and work.

### How does regional policy connect to domestic demand?

Better mobility, public services, logistics, and market integration can raise household confidence and make China's large domestic market work more efficiently.
