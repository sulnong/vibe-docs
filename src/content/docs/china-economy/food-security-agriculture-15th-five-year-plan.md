Food security in China's 15th Five-Year Plan is not a narrow agricultural issue. It is part of the national security framework, the regional development strategy, the rural income strategy, the technology strategy, and the domestic supply-chain strategy.

The plan sets a goal for grain production capacity of around 1.45 trillion jin. But the number is only the surface. Underneath it is a broader system: farmland protection, seed technology, agricultural machinery, irrigation, disaster prevention, reserves, import diversification, county industries, rural public services, and fiscal support for farmers and grain-producing regions.

## The short answer

China's 15th Five-Year Plan treats food security as strategic capacity. It wants stable grain output, stronger agricultural technology, better farmland, more advanced machinery, diversified food supply, higher rural incomes, and more controllable import channels.

The plan's agriculture and food-security agenda has six parts:

| Part | What the plan emphasizes |
| --- | --- |
| Grain capacity | Raise comprehensive grain production capacity to around 1.45 trillion jin and protect basic self-sufficiency in grain. |
| Farmland | Protect cultivated land quantity, quality, and ecology; improve high-standard farmland; protect black soil; use saline-alkali land better. |
| Seed and technology | Strengthen germplasm resources, commercial breeding, seed security, agricultural machinery, and key technology breakthroughs. |
| Diversified food supply | Expand oil crops, soybeans, cotton, sugar, rubber, vegetables, livestock, aquaculture, specialty agriculture, and branded products. |
| Rural revitalization | Improve rural living conditions, county industries, governance, public services, and income channels. |
| Strategic imports | Maintain controllable overseas supply channels and diversify imports for products China cannot or does not want to produce fully at home. |

The plan's core principle is simple: China wants enough domestic capacity to avoid being strategically vulnerable, while still using trade for selected shortages and quality needs.

## Food security is economic security

The plan places food inside both the agricultural modernization section and the national economic security section. That placement matters.

Food security affects:

- inflation;
- household welfare;
- rural income;
- land use;
- local finance;
- fertilizer and energy demand;
- trade exposure;
- geopolitical risk;
- social stability.

China is large enough that dependence on global grain markets can move prices and politics. It also has limited per-capita arable land, uneven water resources, and a large population. That is why the plan keeps food security close to national security language.

## Grain capacity is the anchor

The plan's grain goal is around 1.45 trillion jin of comprehensive production capacity. It also says China should ensure basic self-sufficiency in grains and absolute security in staple grains.

That formulation is important. China does not aim to produce everything domestically. It distinguishes between core grains and other food categories.

The plan emphasizes:

- stable rice and wheat production;
- higher corn and soybean capacity;
- higher yields across grain and oil crops;
- protection of important agricultural products;
- high-standard farmland;
- farmland irrigation and drainage;
- black-soil protection;
- saline-alkali land use;
- agricultural disaster prevention.

This is capacity policy, not just annual output policy. The goal is to make the food system more resilient against weather, trade, price, and geopolitical shocks.

## Seeds and machinery are strategic technologies

The plan's agriculture section has a technology core. It calls for seed-industry revitalization, germplasm protection, commercial breeding systems, joint breeding breakthroughs, new high-yield and stress-resistant varieties, and seed-source security.

It also calls for higher-quality agricultural machinery and a target of 80% comprehensive mechanization for crop plowing, planting, and harvesting.

This places agriculture inside the same technology self-reliance logic that appears in chips, industrial software, AI, and energy. Seeds and agricultural machinery may look less glamorous than AI models, but they are strategic if they determine food output, yield stability, and import exposure.

The plan also names agricultural water-saving technology and agricultural science-and-technology enterprises. That suggests a push toward more industrialized, data-informed, equipment-heavy agriculture.

## Rural revitalization is not only farming

The plan's rural strategy includes more than grain. It calls for livable and workable countryside, county-level industries that enrich residents, rural governance, rural public services, and stronger fiscal and financial support.

This matters because rural policy has two goals that can conflict:

- keep enough people, land, and institutions in agriculture to maintain food security;
- allow labor, income, and public services to modernize as people move and rural economies diversify.

The plan tries to square that circle through county industries, rural services, agricultural branding, tourism, logistics, and better public services. It wants rural areas to remain productive and socially stable, not frozen as low-income agricultural zones.

## Imports are allowed, but dependence is managed

The economic security section says China should establish stable and controllable overseas supply channels, diversify agricultural imports, and import scarce or high-quality products appropriately.

That is a realistic line. China cannot fully insulate its food system from global markets, and it does not need to. But it wants trade to be diversified and manageable.

The plan's food-security logic is therefore not autarky. It is controlled interdependence.

This also links to energy and fertilizer. Food security depends on potash, phosphate, fuel, electricity, machinery, water, logistics, and storage. The plan explicitly mentions fertilizer reserve systems and potash and phosphate supply stability.

## What to watch

The agriculture agenda will be visible in output, local plans, and technology policy. Watch:

- whether grain capacity approaches the 1.45 trillion jin goal;
- whether soybean and corn capacity rises without undermining other crops;
- whether high-standard farmland construction improves real productivity;
- whether seed-industry reform produces commercial gains;
- whether agricultural mechanization reaches small and fragmented farms;
- whether rural counties build viable industries rather than debt-heavy projects;
- whether grain-producing regions receive better fiscal compensation;
- whether import diversification reduces vulnerability without raising costs sharply.

## FAQ

### Why does China emphasize food security so much?

Because food affects inflation, household welfare, rural stability, trade exposure, and national security. China's population size makes food supply a strategic issue.

### Does China want food self-sufficiency?

It wants strong domestic capacity and basic grain self-sufficiency, especially for staple grains. It still plans to use imports for some scarce and high-quality products.

### Why are seeds important?

Seeds affect yield, resilience, quality, and dependence on foreign technology. The plan treats seed security as part of agricultural modernization.

### Is rural revitalization only about farming?

No. It includes county industries, public services, rural governance, logistics, tourism, agricultural branding, and better living conditions.
